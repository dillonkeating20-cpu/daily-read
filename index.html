Simón Bolívar: The Man Who Liberated a Continent — and Lost It Anyway

Venezuela is in crisis again today — twin 7.5-magnitude earthquakes leveling neighborhoods, a government in disarray less than six months after the US seizure of Nicolás Maduro, and a population already hollowed out by years of economic collapse. It's the kind of moment that makes you reach back into Venezuelan history and ask: has it always been this hard? The answer, when you study the man who literally created the country, is an uncomfortable yes. Simón Bolívar didn't just fight for Venezuelan independence — he liberated six nations, invented political systems, and then watched nearly all of it fall apart in his own lifetime. If you want to understand why Latin American states are so often fragile, start here.

The Origin: Born in Caracas in 1783 to a wealthy creole family, Bolívar was orphaned young, educated in Europe, and radicalized by Enlightenment ideas — Rousseau, Locke, Montesquieu. He wasn't a man of the people by birth; he was an aristocrat who chose revolution. He traveled to Napoleon's Europe and watched a single man reshape a continent through will and military genius. The lesson lodged deep. When he returned to Venezuela, he didn't just want independence from Spain — he wanted to build something entirely new. That ambition, magnificent and ultimately self-defeating, defined everything that followed.

The Strategy: Bolívar's genius wasn't just battlefield tactics — it was his understanding that independence required manufactured legitimacy. He was repeatedly defeated and forced into exile (twice). Most men quit. Bolívar reframed each exile as a reset, not a failure. His most audacious move came in 1819: rather than fight Spain in the flatlands where it had advantages, he marched an army of 2,500 men across the freezing, nearly impassable Andes in winter — a move so unexpected it echoes Hannibal crossing the Alps. He descended into New Granada, annihilated a Spanish force at the Battle of Boyacá in August 1819, and liberated what is now Colombia in three days. It remains one of the most stunning strategic gambits in military history. He then repeated the trick, liberating Venezuela, Ecuador, Peru, and finally Upper Peru — which renamed itself Bolivia in his honor.

"A state too expensive in itself, or by virtue of its dependencies, ultimately falls into decay; its free government is transformed into a tyranny; it disregards the principles which it should preserve, and finally degenerates into despotism." — Simón Bolívar, Address to the Congress of Angostura, 1819

The Greatest Achievement: By 1825, Bolívar had personally overseen the liberation of a territory larger than all of Europe combined. He was president of Gran Colombia (modern Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, and Panama), and effectively the dominant political figure in all of South America. He attempted something almost no revolutionary leader ever tries: he voluntarily constrained his own power, writing constitutions, building legal frameworks, attempting to construct republican institutions in places that had never had them. He understood, intellectually, that liberty required structure. He was ahead of his time in saying so.

The Downfall: Here is where it gets brutal and instructive. The very nations Bolívar liberated began fracturing almost immediately. Regional caudillos didn't want a united republic — they wanted their own fiefdoms. His dream of a unified Latin America, Gran Colombia, dissolved in 1830 amid coups, regionalism, and assassination plots. He narrowly escaped murder. Increasingly authoritarian in his final years — because he genuinely believed strong central power was the only way to hold things together — he became a figure his own contemporaries condemned as a would-be dictator. He resigned the presidency and died of tuberculosis in December 1830, aged 47, en route to self-imposed exile in Europe, reportedly saying the continent was ungovernable. He died believing he had failed.

The Legacy: He hadn't failed — not entirely. Every one of those nations still exists. His constitutional frameworks, however imperfect, outlasted him. But the warning encoded in his life is this: you can win the revolution and still lose the republic. Building institutions is orders of magnitude harder than tearing down the old order, and the people most skilled at destruction are rarely the best architects of what comes next. Bolívar knew this. He just couldn't solve it.

Why It Matters Today
Venezuela is once again at a founding moment — Maduro gone, twin earthquakes devastating Caracas, no stable government in sight. Bolívar's life is a direct instruction manual for what comes next, and a warning. The hardest part isn't removing a bad leader. It's building something durable after. The country that bears his dream most directly is, two centuries later, still struggling with the exact tension he never resolved: the pull between strong central authority and genuine self-determination. For anyone building systems — whether nations or startups — his arc is a masterclass in why execution infrastructure matters more than visionary ambition.
Geopolitics World

Venezuela Hit by Back-to-Back Major Quakes as Country Teeters

A 7.2 and a 7.5 magnitude earthquake struck Venezuela within a single minute of each other this week, causing widespread building collapses in Caracas and surrounding areas. The timing is brutal: Venezuela is already in the middle of a political vacuum after the US seizure of Nicolás Maduro less than six months ago, with no stable successor government firmly in place. Natural disasters of this scale require a functioning state response — coordinated emergency services, supply chains, international aid coordination — and Venezuela currently has a compromised version of all three. The international community is watching to see whether this becomes a pure humanitarian catastrophe or a forcing function for accelerated political reconstruction.

The human cost is still being calculated, but the images coming from Caracas outskirts are severe. Infrastructure in Venezuela was already critically degraded before the quakes hit, making collapse risk dramatically higher than it would be in a country with maintained building stock.

US Politics Domestic

Trump Asks Congress for Billions for Iran War — and His Own Party Is the Problem

President Trump has formally asked Congress to authorize billions in funding for the Iran military engagement, but he's running into resistance from within the Republican Party itself — not just Democrats. Several GOP members are balking at the price tag and at what they see as insufficient congressional input on the decision to go to war. This is the classic post-war-authorization squeeze: the executive starts a military operation, then has to go back to the legislature for the money to sustain it, and discovers that political support is far more conditional than it appeared at the outset.

Separately, progressives are on a winning streak in Democratic primaries — most visibly in New York City — and centrist Democrats are now openly alarmed. Heading into midterms, the internal Democratic fracture is becoming as significant a story as Republican infighting. Both parties are fighting two wars simultaneously: one against the other side, one against themselves.
