Historical Leader Saladin

The Sultan Who Understood That War Is Just Politics at Higher Stakes

The Middle East is once again reshaping itself around force, leverage, and the threat of chokepoints. Iran is warning it will close the Strait of Hormuz. Israel is striking Lebanon. A turtle conservationist is dead on a beach she refused to leave. It is a good morning to think about Saladin — a man who fought one of history's most ferocious religious wars and still managed to be remembered as magnanimous by his enemies.

The Rise. Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub was born in 1137 in Tikrit — modern-day Iraq — to a Kurdish military family in the service of a Seljuk governor. He was not born to greatness. His early career was administrative, bookish even. He studied Islamic law and showed little early appetite for the battlefield. What he had, and what would define everything, was an unusually clear-eyed ability to read political terrain. When his uncle Shirkuh was dispatched to Egypt by the Zengid ruler Nur ad-Din to prevent the Crusaders from controlling the Fatimid Caliphate, Saladin went along as a subordinate officer. When Shirkuh died in 1169, Saladin stepped into a vacuum — and within two years had dissolved the Fatimid Caliphate entirely, consolidating Egypt under Sunni rule and positioning himself as the most powerful independent actor in the Muslim world.

His first strategy was not military. It was economic and institutional. He rebuilt Egypt's tax base, reformed its military, and cultivated religious legitimacy with scholars and jurists. He understood something that most conquerors miss: armies are expensive, and the money has to come from somewhere. He built the infrastructure before he went to war.

The Turning Point. July 4, 1187. The Battle of Hattin. Saladin lured the Crusader army — under the reckless King Guy of Lusignan — away from water sources in the brutal summer heat of the Galilee region. He encircled them on a waterless volcanic plateau, lit fires upwind to fill their lungs with smoke, and destroyed the largest Crusader force ever assembled in the Levant in a single afternoon. Within three months, Jerusalem had fallen to him — the city the Crusaders had held for 88 years.

What happened next is the part that still startles historians. Saladin did not massacre the population. He allowed Christian civilians to ransom themselves out. He personally paid the ransoms of those who couldn't afford it. He permitted the Patriarch of Jerusalem to leave with the church's treasures. He is reported to have wept at the sight of mothers separated from children and ordered families reunited. This was not naivety. It was calculated statesmanship — he was building a reputation that would outlive him and make future negotiations easier. He was right. It worked.

"The greatest jihad is to battle your own soul, to fight the evil within yourself." — Saladin

The Downfall and Legacy. The Third Crusade, launched in response to Hattin, brought Richard I of England — a genuinely terrifying military opponent. Saladin and Richard fought to a strategic stalemate. Saladin held Jerusalem, but could not expel the Crusaders from the coast. Richard could not retake Jerusalem but controlled the ports. They negotiated the Treaty of Jaffa in 1192: Christians would retain coastal access and could visit Jerusalem unarmed. It was less than total victory for either man. Saladin died eight months later, in March 1193, having given away most of his personal wealth. His treasury held 47 dirhams. Not enough for his own funeral.

His legacy is a case study in what happens when you combine hard military power with deliberate restraint. He is still, eight centuries later, cited by Arab leaders as the model statesman. His name carries genuine reverence across cultures that agree on almost nothing else.

Why It Matters Today

Iran's threat to close the Strait of Hormuz is textbook chokepoint leverage — the same logic Saladin used at Hattin, cutting off water before the decisive battle. The difference is that Saladin's restraint after Hattin is exactly what gave him durable influence. The actors in today's Middle East are deploying maximum pressure with minimum diplomacy. Saladin's career is a direct argument that the two are not opposites — restraint, deployed strategically, is a force multiplier. The question worth asking: who in today's conflict has the long game mapped out, and who is just reacting?

Geopolitics World Briefing

Iran Threatens to Close the Strait of Hormuz as Lebanon War Escalates

Iran has declared it will close the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow passage through which roughly 20% of the world's oil flows — if Israel continues its strikes in Lebanon. Tehran frames the continued campaign as a violation of its ceasefire understanding with Washington. This is not a bluff to dismiss lightly: a closure would detonate global energy markets almost instantly. The strike also killed Mona Khalil, a Lebanese turtle conservationist who had refused to evacuate the beach she spent years protecting. She becomes a symbol of exactly the kind of civilian cost that Iran is using to justify its escalation posture. The Strait threat is designed to impose economic pain on the West, forcing a reckoning over whether continued support for Israeli operations is worth a potential oil price shock. Watch the next 48–72 hours for US diplomatic back-channel movement.

Trump vs. Meloni: The G7 Photo Row That Tells You Something Real About Alliance Dynamics

Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni — arguably the European leader most ideologically aligned with Trump — told him this week to "focus on your own popularity" after he publicly claimed she "begged" for a photo at the G7 summit. The spat matters beyond the tabloid surface: Meloni is Trump's natural ally in Europe, and if he's publicly humiliating her, it signals either a genuine deterioration of the relationship or a deliberate pattern of dominance-display toward all partners regardless of alignment. Either reading is important for understanding how US foreign policy gets made in this moment — it's increasingly personal, reactive, and status-driven rather than strategically managed. For anyone building internationally or thinking about where regulatory and trade headwinds come from, the volatility at the top of the alliance structure is a real variable, not background noise.
